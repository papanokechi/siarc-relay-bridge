2 From discrete equations to surface theory
The study of discrete Painleve equations is currently the focus of an intensive activity. These discrete Painleve
systems are of interest, as they share many properties in common with the di erential ones, including the
ane Weyl group symmetry, the Lax pair, Hirota's bilinear form and the special solutions. We can add the
simplicity of their algebraic and geometric structures to the reasons why we study discrete systems.
    Introduction of the singularity con nement method brought forth intriguing developments [22]. Rational
mappings of a discrete equation may have poles which give rise to in nities at particular input values of
solutions. The singularity con nement demands that after a nite iteration of steps these in nities disappear
and memory of the input data be recovered [9]. We can regard it as a discrete counterpart of Painleve
property. In this section we review brie y the singularity con nement method taking the sixth q-Painleve
equation (q-PV I ) as an example, and give a geometric interpretation to it in non-technical terms.

5. Let us mention further related problems.
    A natural question that comes to mind is whether our approach is related to monodromy preserving
deformations. We imagine that there may be a correspondence between some invariants of di erential
equations and the family X in this paper.
    Another problem is an extention to the multi-variable cases. The Painleve equations have Cremona
transformations as their origin. We believe that a similar correspondence between birational geometry and
integrable systems exists in higher dimensional situations as well, though algebraic geometry becomes more
dicult. Theory of generalized del Pezzo varieties [5] may give us some hints to this idea.
    A di erent approach to the Painleve equations also based on the ane symmetry and discrete systems has
been developed by M. Noumi and Y. Yamada [18]. They constructed representations of ane Weyl groups
on the eld of rational functions and found a new presentation of the Painleve equations. This formulation
makes the symmetry apparent and allows for straightforward extention to the higher dimensional case. Our
approach o ers a geometric characterization of various structures of the Painleve equations, while their
theory provides an e ective algorithmic tool for studying them.

1. As is well known, the Painleve di erential equations have two independent origins. The rst is due
to P. Painleve and coworkers [21, 8]. In their original work, the Painleve equations were discovered as
di erential equations whose solutions do not have movable singularities other than poles. This feature, now
called the Painleve property, has become a guiding principle in the theory of integrable systems. The second
is the monodromy preserving deformation of linear di erential equations [7, 12]. Given a system of linear
di erential equations, the problem is to deform it in such a way that the monodromy group stays constant.
Here the Painleve equations arise as the condition for the invariance of the monodromy, written in terms of
the coecients of the underlying linear system.
    Our aim in this paper is to characterize the Painleve equations from yet another point of view: theory of
rational surfaces. Before entering into the detailed discussions, we begin by reviewing previous works which
directly motivated the present study. In the following, we shall follow the standard notation PI ; 1 1 1 ; PV I to
represent the six types of the Painleve equations.

        We present a geometric approach to the theory of Painleve equations based on rational surfaces. Our
    starting point is a compact smooth rational surface X which has a unique anti-canonical divisor D of
